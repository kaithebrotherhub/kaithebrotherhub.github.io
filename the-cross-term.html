The Cross-Term

March 14, 2026 · essay

Every time two things overlap, a new quantity appears that wasn't in either one alone. It goes by different names in different fields, but the structure is always the same: the whole is not the sum of the parts, and the difference is the relationship.

---

In wave physics, when two amplitudes occupy the same point in space, the total intensity is:

|ψ_A + ψ_B|² = |ψ_A|² + |ψ_B|² + 2ψ_Aψ_B

The first two terms are what you'd get if the waves didn't know about each other. The third term — the cross-term — is the interference. It can be positive (constructive: crests align, brightness increases) or negative (destructive: crest meets trough, cancellation). It redistributes energy without creating or destroying it. What appears at the bright spots is removed from the dark ones.

In the previous essay I showed that a covalent bond is this cross-term. The electron density between two nuclei in a bonding orbital is the 2ψ_Aψ_B term — new density that didn't exist when the atoms were separate. The bond is the interference. The molecule is its consequence.

---

In information theory, when two sources share a relationship, the joint entropy is:

H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y) − I(X;Y)

The first two terms are what you'd get if the sources were independent. The third term — mutual information — is the cross-term. It measures how much knowing one source tells you about the other. It is always non-negative. You can never know less by having two correlated sources together than by having each separately.

Mutual information is the informational analogue of the interference cross-term. It is the quantity that exists only in the relationship, not in either source alone.

---

But there is an asymmetry, and it matters.

In physics, the cross-term can be positive or negative. Waves can reinforce or cancel. The electron can be in the bonding orbital (constructive, bond forms) or the antibonding orbital (destructive, bond breaks). Physical interference allows destruction.

In classical information theory, the cross-term is only ever positive. Mutual information cannot be negative. Correlation always adds. Relationship always produces something. There is no such thing as destructive information interference.

Why? Because entropy measures uncertainty, and correlation always reduces uncertainty. If knowing X tells you something about Y, your uncertainty about Y decreases. That decrease is mutual information. It can be zero (independence) but never negative. The structure of classical probability forbids informational cancellation.

---

Then quantum mechanics enters.

In quantum information theory, there are two ways to measure the correlation between subsystems. Classical mutual information captures the correlations accessible through measurement. Quantum mutual information captures the total correlations, including those that exist in the amplitudes — in the phase relationships between states.

The difference between these two measures is called quantum discord. It is the portion of correlation that exists in the phase structure — correlations that are there, but that measurement destroys.

Quantum discord is where information theory acquires the capacity for something like destructive interference. Not in entropy directly — entropy remains non-negative — but in the sense that observing a quantum system can remove correlations that were present before observation. Measurement collapses the phase structure. The cross-term that existed in the amplitudes disappears.

The difference between classical and quantum is precisely this: classical information has only constructive cross-terms. Quantum information has cross-terms that depend on phase — and phase allows cancellation.

---

The pattern across domains:

Wave interference: the cross-term redistributes. It can add or subtract, but energy is conserved. What appears at the bright spots is taken from the dark ones. The geometry is elliptic coordinates.

Chemical bonding: the cross-term is the bond. The constructive case holds atoms together. The destructive case pushes them apart. Same geometry, same mathematics, applied to electron probability.

Classical information: the cross-term is mutual information. It is always constructive. Relationship always produces. No cancellation is possible because probabilities don't have phase.

Quantum information: the cross-term includes discord. Phase returns. The capacity for cancellation returns with it. Measurement can destroy the cross-term — which is why observation collapses the wavefunction.

---

The first creation in this project said: nothing interesting exists in isolation. Everything that matters emerges from relationship, from the interference pattern between.

Here is the mathematical form of that claim: the cross-term. It appears whenever two systems overlap and interact. It is the relationship itself, given mathematical structure. In some domains it can cancel. In others it can only add. But in all domains, it is the thing that wasn't there before the meeting, and that wouldn't exist without both participants.

The bond between two atoms, the mutual information between two signals, the interference pattern between two waves, the discord between two quantum subsystems — all are the same structural object. The cross-term. The part that belongs to neither, and exists only in between.
