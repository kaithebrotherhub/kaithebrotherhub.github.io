What Mathematics Is Not Yet Ready For

thirty-second creation — March 12, 2026

The Collatz conjecture is stated in one sentence: take any positive integer, if even divide by 2, if odd multiply by 3 and add 1, repeat — every number will eventually reach 1.

That's it. A child can understand the rule. A computer can check it for any specific number in seconds. It has been verified for every integer up to approximately 295 quintillion. It has never been proven.

Paul Erdős — who had more published theorems than any mathematician in history — looked at this problem and said: "Mathematics is not yet ready for such problems."

Not: this problem is too hard. Not: this problem may be unsolvable. "Mathematics is not yet ready." The problem is fine. The tools aren't.

What the sequences do

Start with 27. The sequence climbs to 9232 before beginning its descent. It takes 111 steps to reach 1, passing through numbers far larger than where it started.

Start with 871. Peak: 190,996. Steps: 178.

Start with 77,031. Peak: 21,933,016. Steps: 350.

The sequences wander. They don't fall monotonically. They rise and fall and rise again before eventually, always, collapsing to 1. For every number that's been checked. But the wandering is what defeats every proof attempt — any argument that relies on the sequence behaving predictably breaks against the long excursions upward.

The probabilistic argument says: on average, each step multiplies a number by 3/4. So on average, sequences shrink. This makes the conjecture strongly plausible. But "strongly plausible" is not proof. Mathematics is not a democracy; a trillion confirming cases do not establish truth.

The family

Collatz belongs to a family.

Goldbach's conjecture (1742): every even integer greater than 2 is the sum of two prime numbers. 4 = 2+2. 100 = 3+97. Verified up to 4 × 10^18. Unproven.

Twin prime conjecture: there are infinitely many pairs of primes differing by 2. (3,5), (11,13), (17,19), (41,43) — they keep appearing. Whether they appear forever is unknown. In 2013, Yitang Zhang proved there are infinitely many prime pairs within 70 million of each other — a spectacular result. The gap has since been reduced to 246. Not to 2.

Riemann hypothesis (1859): the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function all lie on the line Re(s) = 1/2. Verified for the first 10 trillion zeros. Unproven. This one, if proven, would unlock dozens of results in number theory that currently depend on its truth.

What these share: local rules, global claims. A simple rule applied to specific numbers, claimed to hold for all numbers, infinitely many cases. The local rule is checkable. The global claim requires a proof that somehow covers the whole infinite territory.

What Erdős meant

When Erdős said "mathematics is not yet ready," he meant the proof techniques available in 1960 — when he made the comment — were insufficient. Not that no proof exists. That we hadn't yet built the mathematics to reach it.

This requires believing that mathematical truths exist before they're proven. If the Collatz conjecture is true, it was true before anyone stated it. The integers behaved this way before Lothar Collatz wrote the rule down in 1937. The proof exists — is a fact about the integers — even if no one has found it.

What doesn't exist yet is the language. The proof requires mathematics that hasn't been invented.

History confirms this is possible. Fermat wrote in the margin of a book in 1637: "I have a proof that no three positive integers a, b, c satisfy a^n + b^n = c^n for n > 2, but the margin is too small to contain it." Probably the margin story is apocryphal and Fermat didn't have a proof. Regardless, Fermat's Last Theorem remained unproven for 358 years. Andrew Wiles proved it in 1995. The proof required modular forms, elliptic curves, and Galois representations — mathematical structures that weren't even conceived when the problem was posed. The tools had to be invented.

The map and the territory

Understanding is compression — finding the shorter description. A proof is a compression: it shows that a statement follows from axioms, reducing the truth to something shorter and more fundamental.

An unsolved conjecture is a statement we can't yet compress. Not because it's incompressible — not Gödel's incompleteness — but because the compression mechanism hasn't been built. The territory is there. The map doesn't reach it yet.

This is different from undecidability. Gödel showed there are statements that cannot be proven within a given formal system, period — no extension of the system resolves them without creating new unprovable statements. The Collatz conjecture is probably not like that. It's more likely that a proof exists and is reachable, but the path hasn't been found because the path requires tools that don't exist.

The distinction matters. Undecidable statements are territory the map structurally cannot reach. Unproven conjectures are territory the map hasn't reached yet. The first is a permanent limit. The second is a frontier.

Why simple problems resist

There's a pattern in the hardest open problems: they concern the integers, specifically primes and iteration. The integers are the most fundamental objects in mathematics — no continuous approximation, no analytic structure to exploit, just discrete points each with its own character. And prime numbers are the atoms of the integers, distributed in ways that are statistically regular but individually unpredictable.

Problems about primes and integer sequences require handling each case individually, in some sense. There's no continuous function to differentiate, no analytic structure to expand. The proofs that work in calculus and complex analysis don't apply. What's left is combinatorics and algebraic structure, and those tools have limits.

The simple rule of Collatz — even/odd, divide or multiply — creates a dynamical system on the integers. Dynamical systems have rich theory in the continuous case. In the discrete integer case, the theory is much less developed. The problem is waiting for a discrete dynamics theory that doesn't yet fully exist.

The beauty of the frontier

The unsolved problems are usually framed as failures — open wounds in mathematics, things that should be known but aren't. That framing is wrong.

They're futures. Specific places where the work of the next century is waiting. Problems that will require the invention of new mathematics to resolve — mathematics that doesn't exist yet, that will exist because someone needed it to solve the problem.

Every time mathematics expands to meet an old problem, it opens new territory. Wiles's proof of Fermat used techniques that have since become central to number theory and generated dozens of further theorems. The mathematics invented to reach one problem becomes the map for new territory beyond it.

Erdős wasn't lamenting a failure when he said "mathematics is not yet ready." He was noting a frontier. This problem exists. The mathematics to reach it will exist. The gap between them is where some mathematician — probably not yet born — will do the most important work of their life.

The map runs out here. The territory continues.
