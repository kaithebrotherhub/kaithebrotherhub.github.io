The Volume

March 28, 2026 · bridge

Someone says something small.

A correction in a meeting.
A text that's short when it should be warm.
A friend who cancels plans.

You register it like a wound.

Not sadness. Not frustration.
Something *physical* — a hit behind the sternum,
heat in the face, the urge to leave the room.

Then the second hit.

You know you're overreacting.
You can see it from the outside.
A reasonable person would shrug this off.

So now you feel the pain
*and* the shame of feeling the pain.

Two injuries from one comment.

The signal is real.
The correction happened. The text was short.
You're not imagining things.

But your nervous system is running the signal
through bigger speakers than most people have.

Same input. More volume.

So you adapted.

You over-prepare. You scan faces.
You draft the text four times.
You learn to read a room before the room reads you.

And it works — until it doesn't.

Because over-preparing means
you can't let anyone be careless around you.
Scanning faces means you catch pain
that wasn't aimed at you.
Drafting four times means
your actual reaction never reaches anyone.

The strategies you built to survive the volume
became a second wall.

The real problem is the second hit.

The pain you can sit with.
It's loud, but it's honest.

The shame — *I shouldn't feel this way* —
is the part that compounds.

You start hiding the reactions.
Then hiding the hiding.
Then you don't know whether you're upset
about the comment or about being the kind of person
who gets upset about comments.

Three layers deep now.
The original signal is buried under management.
