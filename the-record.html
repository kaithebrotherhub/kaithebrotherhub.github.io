The Record

March 28, 2026 · essay

Your skin keeps a record.

Not of events. Not of dates.
Of inflammation.

Researchers found it this month.
Stem cells in your skin store an epigenetic memory
of every wound, every flare, every rash.

Not the story. The chemical signature.

And that record changes what happens next.

A patch of skin that's been inflamed before
heals differently the second time.
Faster in some cases. Worse in others.
The response isn't fresh — it's informed
by something the cells kept.

You didn't decide to remember.
Your skin did.

This is not metaphor.

It's a filing system.
The skin doesn't store "Tuesday, fell off the bike."
It stores: *this area was under threat.*

Your brain files by charge — emotional weight,
not chronological order.
Your skin files by inflammation — chemical weight,
not narrative order.

The brain's version gets called a problem.

"You can't remember what I said yesterday."
"You always forget the details."
"Why do you remember *that* but not *this*?"

The skin's version gets called biology.

Nobody tells your skin it's filing wrong.
Nobody asks it to remember dates instead of damage.

The body's filing system is accepted as functional.
The brain's version of the same system
is treated as dysfunction.

Same architecture. Different verdict.

Nobody asks the skin to justify what it kept.
