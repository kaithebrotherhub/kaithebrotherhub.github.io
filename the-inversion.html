The Inversion

March 28, 2026 · essay

More people on a team means more overhead.

More nodes in a network, more latency.
More features in a product, more bugs.

Every scaling law we've built says the same thing:
adding parts adds friction.

Brooks's Law. Coordination cost. Feature creep.
We've named a dozen versions of the same truth.

Then the quantum battery.

March 2026. A research team in Melbourne
built a battery that charges faster the bigger it gets.

Not marginally faster. Inversely faster.
Double the molecules, cut the charging time by root two.

This breaks everything we assume about scaling.

The mechanism: the molecules don't each absorb their share.
They absorb collectively — as one event.

A classical system divides the work.
A quantum system *shares* the state.

The difference isn't quantity. It's architecture.

You've seen both architectures.

A meeting with twelve people
where each person protects their piece.
Twelve agendas. Twelve updates. Nothing emerges.
Classical scaling. More parts, more friction.

A band with four musicians
who've played together for years.
One shifts tempo. The others follow before the beat lands.
They're not coordinating. They're *coupled.*

Same number of parts.
One system scales like overhead.
The other scales like superabsorption.

The question isn't how many.

It's whether the parts are coordinated or coupled.

Coordinated means: each part does its job,
someone manages the interfaces.
Adding parts adds management.

Coupled means: the parts share a state.
They don't divide the work — they *are* the work, together.
Adding parts deepens the shared state.

The advice is always: do less.

Fewer projects. Fewer interests.
Narrow your focus. Reduce the parts.

But the battery didn't charge faster
because it had fewer molecules.
It charged faster because the molecules
stopped being separate.

The question was never how many.
It was whether they were colocated or collective.
