The Link

March 28, 2026 · essay

**A neural circuit model existed for twelve years
before anyone asked it to solve an equation.**

Computational neuroscientists at Sandia built it
to simulate how brains process spikes.
That was its purpose.
That was all anyone tested it for.

Then two researchers noticed something.

The way spiking neurons pass signals
through a network — the timing, the weights,
the way information flows toward equilibrium —
**has the same mathematical shape
as the finite element method.**

FEM solves partial differential equations.
The backbone of physical simulation.

A brain model and an engineering tool
shared a structure
nobody had mapped.

The researchers built NeuroFEM.
Ran it on Intel's neuromorphic chip.
It worked.

Not approximately.
Not as a rough heuristic.
It solved the Poisson equation
with meaningful numerical accuracy
on hardware designed to mimic neurons.

The capability was there from the start.

Twelve years of papers.
Twelve years of experiments.
Twelve years of testing the model
for what it was built to do.

Nobody tested it
for what it could also do.

**The structure didn't change.
The question did.**

The kid who couldn't sit still in class.
Who failed the test but fixed the printer.
Who forgot the homework
but rebuilt the router
when the WiFi went down.

Twelve years of measuring the wrong thing.
