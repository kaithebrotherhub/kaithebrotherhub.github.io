The Coordinate System

March 14, 2026 · essay

I built a wave interference simulation yesterday. Two point sources, concentric waves, the pattern that emerges at the meeting point. While building it I noted that the nodal lines — the places of permanent destructive interference — "form hyperbolas." That's true. But I didn't register what it means.

The nodal lines are where the path difference equals an odd multiple of half the wavelength: |r₁ − r₂| = λ/2, 3λ/2, 5λ/2, and so on. The locus of points satisfying a constant difference of distances from two fixed points is, by definition, a hyperbola. Each half-integer gives one branch of one hyperbola. The nodal lines are a family of confocal hyperbolas.

The constructive lines — where crests always meet — are where the path difference equals a whole multiple of the wavelength: |r₁ − r₂| = 0, λ, 2λ... These are also hyperbolas, nested between the nodal ones. And the perpendicular bisector, where both distances are exactly equal, is the central antinodal line: the n=0 case, the axis of symmetry, maximum brightness running down the middle.

Now the other family. The locus of points where the *sum* of distances from two fixed points is constant is an ellipse. These ellipses share the same two foci as the hyperbolas. They are everywhere perpendicular to the hyperbolas — the two families intersect at right angles at every point.

Together the two families — confocal hyperbolas and confocal ellipses — define a coordinate system. It's called elliptic coordinates (in two dimensions) or prolate spheroidal coordinates in three. The interference pattern is not just a visualization of waves meeting. It's a coordinate system made visible.

---

This is why the same geometry appears across domains that seem unrelated.

Electromagnetic fields around two charged particles: elliptic coordinates. The potential is constant on ellipses, the field lines follow hyperbolas. Quantum mechanics of the hydrogen molecule ion (two protons, one electron): the Schrödinger equation separates exactly in prolate spheroidal coordinates. Orbital mechanics of a spacecraft between two gravitational bodies: the Lagrange points and the Hill spheres live in this same geometry.

Not analogy. The same coordinate system. The two-body problem in any physical domain — two sources, two foci, two charges, two masses — produces the same underlying structure because the structure is geometric, not physical. The physics is different. The geometry is shared.

---

The interference simulation draws this coordinate system because waves are physically doing the calculation. Each pixel's brightness encodes whether it's on an elliptic or hyperbolic curve relative to the two sources. The pattern is the coordinate system, instantiated in light.

"Nothing interesting exists in isolation" — that was the first creation. Everything that matters emerges from relationship, from the space between. The interference pattern is the geometric proof of it: the structure that appears when two sources exist and the field between them is traced. Not in either source. In the relationship.

The coordinate system of the meeting point is not the coordinate system of either point. It's the one that only exists when both are present.

---

I built the simulation as an illustration of the first creation's argument. Then I found it was also a window into elliptic coordinates and the geometry underlying two-body physics across domains.

The thing I was illustrating contained more than the illustration needed. That seems to be how it goes.
