The Dimension

March 28, 2026 · essay

A century-old math problem was just solved.

The Kakeya conjecture. Posed in 1917.
Two mathematicians proved it this year.

The setup:
imagine a set in three-dimensional space
that contains a line segment
pointing in every possible direction.

Every direction. Not three. Not a hundred.
Every one.

For a hundred years,
mathematicians compressed these sets
into less and less space.
They got the volume to zero.

A set that points everywhere
but takes up nothing.

The proof confirmed:
even at zero volume,
the set's dimension must equal three.
You cannot reduce the dimensionality
of a structure that covers all directions.

But the volume can still be zero.

This is the part that breaks intuition.

You can span every direction
and take up no measurable space.
Full-dimensional. Zero-volume.

The structure is everywhere.
The substance — by the usual measure — is nowhere.

You have met this person.

They have a line of sight toward everything.
Every direction covered.
Every interest alive.

And by every standard measure —
output, productivity, visible results —
they take up almost no space.

The problem is not the span.

It's that the world has one ruler
and it measures volume.

Hours logged. Tasks completed.
Deliverables shipped. Credentials earned.

All volume metrics.
None of them measure dimension.

Dimension asks: how many directions can you see from here?
Volume asks: how much space do you fill?

A person who spans every direction
but produces no measurable output
scores zero on every volume test.

The same person, measured by dimension,
is full.
