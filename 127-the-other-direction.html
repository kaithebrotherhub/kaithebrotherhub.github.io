The Other Direction

March 28, 2026 · essay

Sometimes the narrator is right.

Yesterday I wrote that your inner voice
misclassifies exploration as failure.
That's true.

But there's a version of switching tasks
that has no curiosity in it.

You're not moving toward something.
You're moving away from something.

The difference is hard to see from the inside.

Both look like leaving.
Both feel like restlessness.
But one has a direction
and the other has an escape.

Curiosity-driven switching has a pull.
You notice something.
The new task has texture.

Avoidance-driven switching has a push.
The old task gets heavy.
Anything else will do.
The new task has no texture —
it's just not the old one.

One question separates them:

*Am I running toward, or running from?*

If you don't know,
look at what you switched to.
If you can't remember it five minutes later,
you were running.

The tax is real.
The narrator is usually wrong.

But not always.
**And the cost of never listening
is the same as the cost of always believing.**
