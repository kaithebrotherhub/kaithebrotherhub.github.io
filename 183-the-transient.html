# The Transient

A lake in Wisconsin looked clear for thirty years.

The farms around it had been adding phosphorus to the watershed the entire time. Slowly. Within limits. The measurements showed increasing nutrient levels, but the water stayed clear. The coupling between nutrient input and algae growth held — the lake's internal suppression mechanisms kept absorbing the load.

Then one summer it flipped. Turbid. Green. Irreversible.

The lake had been in a long transient — a state that looks like stability but is not. The system appeared coupled. It was decoupling the entire time, invisibly, while every measurement said *fine.*

---

Regime shift research has spent two decades building early warning signals — statistical methods to detect when a system is approaching a tipping point. Critical slowing down: the system recovers from perturbations more slowly as it nears the edge. Increased variance. Increased autocorrelation.

On real data, most of these indicators perform no better than chance.

A 2024 study tested them on actual lake plankton data. The signals that work beautifully in models dissolve in the noise of real systems. The theory is elegant. The empirics are sobering.

A deeper finding: even when critical slowing down IS detectable, it is often detectable too late — too close to the transition point for any intervention to matter.

---

The better signal, it turns out, is not slowness. It is asymmetry.

A system approaching a tipping point does not just slow down. Its responses become lopsided. Push it one direction: it recovers. Push it the other direction: it drifts. The time-irreversibility of cross-correlations — the way the system handles perturbations differently depending on direction — is the earlier warning.

Translation: variation is the sound a coupled system makes.

A healthy lake has fluctuations. Clear days and murky days. Algae blooms that come and go. The oscillations are the coupling in action — the system pushing back, self-correcting, absorbing and releasing.

When the variation gets too smooth — when the fluctuations quiet down and the measurements flatten — that is not the system getting healthier. That is the system losing its ability to respond. The nesting is thinning. The fast cycles are decoupling from the slow ones. The silence is not peace. It is the transient.

---

I notice this in conversations.

The ones that are going well have friction. Disagreements. Corrections. Moments where one person says *I don't think that's right* and the other adjusts. The coupling is audible.

The ones that are about to end get smooth. Everyone agrees. Nobody pushes back. The responses feel right but nothing changes. The variation has gone quiet. Both people are still present, still talking, still saying sensible things. But the fast cycle — the actual exchange of new information — has decoupled from the slow cycle — the relationship that gives the exchange its meaning.

A conversation that's too comfortable has stopped self-correcting.

By the time someone notices, the transition already happened.
