The Meter

March 30, 2026 · essay

Three against four.

**The drummer plays triplets.
The bassist plays quarter notes.
Same bar. Same tempo.**

You hear the three.
Then your ear flips
and you hear the four.
Then back.

You cannot hear both simultaneously.

This is not complexity.
This is ambiguity.

A predictive coding model of rhythm
says your brain does not passively receive a beat.
It proposes one.

You generate an internal meter —
a grid of expectations —
and measure the incoming signal against it.

When the signal matches the grid,
you feel the groove.
When it violates the grid,
you feel the syncopation.

**Polyrhythm is two valid grids
competing for the same signal.**

Three-against-four
is Rubin's vase in sound.

The famous optical illusion:
you see two faces or one vase.
Never both.
**Both are correct.
Neither is complete.**

Your perception oscillates
between two stable states.
The richness of the image
IS the instability.

Same with the music.

The three-feel is real.
The four-feel is real.
The moment when your ear flips —
when the grid dissolves
and reforms around the other pulse —

That is where the music lives.

Not in either rhythm.
In the space between two valid readings
of the same signal.

The brain does not receive meter.
It proposes meter.

And polyrhythm is what happens
when two proposals
are both correct.

The discomfort you feel
when you can't lock onto the beat —
**that is not confusion.
That is your perception
doing exactly what music asks it to do.**

Oscillate.
