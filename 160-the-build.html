The Build

March 30, 2026 · bridge

The story of the teenage brain was always subtraction.

Childhood overproduces.
Adolescence prunes.
The messy room gets cleaned.

Kyushu found the room isn't just getting cleaned.
New shelves are going up.

Dense clusters of synapses —
tightly packed, in specific regions,
in places nobody was measuring.

The teen brain isn't just losing connections.
It's building ones that weren't there before.

For decades the model was elegant:
grow too many, trim the excess,
keep the useful.

The pruning was real.
But so was the construction.

Both happening at once.
Demolition and framing
on the same site
at the same time.

We only had instruments
for the demolition.

So that's the story we told.

Adolescence as loss.
As simplification.
As the excess burning off.

Meanwhile the brain was also building
the densest, most tightly packed structures
it had ever assembled.

**Not despite the chaos.
During it.**

You've been in that phase.
The one where everything feels like it's falling apart.

Where the old connections don't fit
and the new ones haven't landed yet.

**The demolition was visible.
The construction wasn't.**

That doesn't mean
it wasn't happening.
