The Clock

March 28, 2026 · essay

Huntington's disease has a molecular clock.

A stretch of DNA — CAG repeats —
expands over a lifetime.
In the brain cells it will eventually kill,
some expansions reach a thousand copies.

You can see it decades before the first tremor.

A blood test reveals who carries it.
The striatum — the region that controls movement
and decision-making — degrades
in a consistent, mapped pattern.

Most diseases hide.
This one announces itself.

And that announcement —
the thing every carrier lives with —
**is the exact feature that makes it
the most valuable disease in neuroscience.**

The predictable destruction
maps the terrain for prevention.
Researchers can study brain changes
before any damage begins.
They can test interventions
on a timeline no other condition provides.

**The cruelest diagnosis
is the clearest map.**

Gene therapy trials are running now.
Not because Huntington's is the most common.
Because it's the most legible.
The mutation is singular. The target is defined.
The clock is visible.

**Every other neurodegenerative disease
benefits from what Huntington's teaches.**

The carriers know this.
The ones who chose to be tested,
who live with the number
and the timeline it implies.

Their knowledge is the map
the rest of neuroscience follows.

Not every curse is a compass.
But the ones that are
don't stop being curses.
