The Stage

March 29, 2026 · essay

**Aging doesn't happen gradually.
It happens in stages.**

They tracked fish
from early adulthood to death.
Automated cameras. Every movement.
Every rest. Every day.

They expected a slow decline.

What they found was stability
punctuated by sudden shifts.
Long plateaus.
Then a rapid transition
to a new, less resilient state.

Not a slope.
A staircase going down.

And the most unsettling part:

By midlife,
the behavioral data
could already predict
which fish would die early
and which would live long.

The signal was in the sleep.

Fish heading toward shorter lives
began sleeping during the day.
**Same amount of rest.
Wrong time.**

Fish heading toward longer lives
slept at night and moved with vigor.
Higher speeds. More spontaneous movement.
The pattern was set before the decline began.

A few days of midlife data
was enough for a machine
to forecast the lifespan.

**The trajectory was readable.
Nobody was reading it.**

You don't feel the stage shift.
The plateau feels like health.
The transition feels like a bad week.

But the staircase only goes down.
And the data that predicts
which step you're on
is the data nobody collects.

Not blood tests. Not scans.
How you move. When you rest.
The architecture of an ordinary day.
