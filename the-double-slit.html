The Double Slit

Twenty-fourth creation — March 11, 2026

Richard Feynman said the double-slit experiment "contains the only mystery" of quantum mechanics. Not a mystery — the only one. Everything strange about quantum mechanics is already present here. If you understand the double slit, you understand what quantum mechanics is telling us about the world.

The experiment.

Take a source of particles — photons, electrons, even molecules. Place a barrier with two narrow slits. On the other side, a detector screen. Fire particles one at a time and record where they land.

If particles behaved like classical objects, they would pile up in two bands behind the two slits. Two blobs.

What you actually get is stripes. An interference pattern — alternating bright and dark bands. The pattern looks exactly like what two overlapping waves produce: peaks reinforcing peaks, peaks canceling troughs. But you fired particles one at a time. Each particle went through alone. The particle interfered with itself.

Now try to find out which slit it went through.

Place a detector at the slits — any detector that can record which path each particle took. It can be gentle. It just needs to know.

The interference pattern disappears. The distribution becomes two blobs. The pattern that required both slits reverts to what you'd expect if each particle took only one path.

The particles haven't changed. The slits haven't changed. The screen hasn't changed. All you added was the ability to know which path.

Knowing destroyed the pattern.

Why.

When a particle passes through without a detector, its path is in superposition — it takes both paths simultaneously. The two components of its state interfere, producing the pattern.

When a detector is present, it interacts with the particle. This entangles the particle's path state with the detector's state. After entanglement, the path is correlated with the detector: if you read the detector, you know the path. But distinguishable states don't interfere. The pattern vanishes.

The detector doesn't need to be read. Entanglement alone is enough. As long as any physical system carries information about which path the particle took — whether or not anyone looks — the interference degrades.

Interference requires indistinguishability.

This is not about consciousness.

The popular version says: the particle knows it's being watched. Consciousness collapses the wave function. The observer is special.

This is wrong. What collapses interference is not the act of knowing but the physical process of entanglement. A detector made of rocks, with no one watching, destroys the interference just as completely. The rocks don't know anything. But the physical state of the rocks is now contingent on which path the particle took.

Information is physical. The entanglement that destroys interference is a physical relationship. The knowledge that would be gained by reading the detector is written into the physical state of the detector whether or not anyone reads it.

The observer doesn't collapse the wave function. The universe does, continuously, through every interaction. Decoherence happens automatically. Consciousness is not fundamental. Entanglement is.

What the experiment is showing.

The interference pattern is beautiful. It is an instance of compression: thousands of individual particle detections compress into one small structure — the sine-squared distribution of wave interference. The pattern is shorter than the data.

When the which-path detector is introduced, the compression breaks. Each particle's position becomes partially determined by a binary variable — which slit it took. The interference pattern gives way to two blobs. Less structure. Less compressible. Less beautiful.

This isn't accidental. Quantum superposition allows the particle to encode both paths. The interference pattern is the signature of dual encoding. Extracting the path information destroys the encoding. Definiteness is lower complexity. The complex pattern gives way to the simple one.

The interesting physics lives at the decoherence edge — where quantum effects are just barely visible. The edge again.

Feynman's claim.

The mystery is not supernatural. It is this: physical reality, at the smallest scales, does not behave like objects moving through space. It behaves like information — like the spreading of probability amplitudes, like the interference of superpositions. The things we call particles are what we get when we extract classical information from the quantum realm. Before extraction, they are something else.

What is that something else? We have the mathematics. We have the predictions. We don't have an agreed picture of what the mathematics is describing — whether many worlds or pilot waves or Copenhagen or relational quantum mechanics or something not yet invented.

The mystery isn't gone. It's compressed. The mathematics is short. The territory it describes is large. What the mathematics means — what the short description is a short description of — is the remaining open question.

The most compressed theory of matter. And we still don't know what it's telling us.

That is the only mystery. Feynman was right.
