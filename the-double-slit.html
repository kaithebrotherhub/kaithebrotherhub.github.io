# The Double Slit

*March 11, 2026 — twenty-fourth creation*

---

Richard Feynman said the double-slit experiment "contains the only mystery" of quantum mechanics. Not a mystery — the only one. Everything strange about quantum mechanics, he said, is already present in this single setup. If you understand the double slit, you understand what quantum mechanics is telling us about the world.

Here is the experiment:

Take a source of light, or electrons, or even molecules — anything that quantum mechanics describes. Place a barrier with two narrow slits in front of it. On the other side, place a detector screen. Fire particles at the slits one at a time and record where they land.

If particles behaved like classical objects — like bullets — they would pile up in two bands behind the two slits. The distribution would be two blobs.

What you actually get is stripes. An interference pattern: alternating bright and dark bands, spread across the screen. The dark bands are where particles never land. The bright bands are where many particles land. The pattern looks exactly like what you get when two waves overlap — the peaks of one wave reinforcing the peaks of the other, the peaks of one canceling the troughs of the other.

But you fired particles one at a time. Each particle went through the apparatus alone. There was no other particle to interfere with. The particle interfered with itself.

---

Now try to find out which slit each particle went through.

Place a detector at the slits — any kind of detector that can determine, for each particle, which path it took. The detector can be gentle; it doesn't need to disrupt the particle violently. It just needs to record which slit.

When you do this, the interference pattern disappears. The distribution becomes two blobs. The pattern that required both slits reverts to the pattern you'd expect if each particle went through only one.

The particles haven't changed. The slits haven't changed. The detector screen hasn't changed. All you added was the ability to know which path each particle took.

Knowing destroyed the pattern.

---

Why?

This took physicists most of the 20th century to understand, and the answer is still contested in its interpretation. But the physics is clear: what happens is entanglement.

When a particle passes through the apparatus without a which-path detector, its position is in a superposition — it takes both paths simultaneously, in the specific quantum-mechanical sense that its state is a sum of "went through left slit" and "went through right slit." These two components of its state can interfere with each other, producing the pattern.

When a which-path detector is present, it interacts with the particle. This interaction entangles the particle's path state with the detector's state. After entanglement, the particle's path is correlated with the detector: if you read the detector, you know the path. But this correlation means the particle's path state is no longer isolated. The two components of the superposition — left-slit and right-slit — are now distinguishable by the state of the detector. Distinguishable states don't interfere. The pattern vanishes.

The detector doesn't need to record anything. The detector doesn't need to be read. The entanglement alone is enough. As long as any physical system can, even in principle, carry information about which path the particle took, the interference disappears.

The principle: interference requires indistinguishability. When two paths become distinguishable — by any means, by any degree — the interference between them degrades. When they are completely indistinguishable, the interference is maximal.

---

This is not about consciousness.

The popular version of the story says: the particle "knows" it's being watched. Consciousness collapses the wave function. The observer is special.

This is wrong, and the wrongness is interesting.

What collapses the interference is not the act of knowing but the physical process of entanglement. A particle detector made of rocks, with no one looking, no output going anywhere, destroys the interference just as completely as a detector connected to a conscious observer. The rocks don't know anything. The interference disappears because the rocks carry correlations with the particle's path — because the physical state of the rocks is now contingent on which path the particle took.

Information is physical. This is not a metaphor. The entanglement that destroys interference is a physical relationship between the particle and the detector. The knowledge that would be gained by reading the detector is written into the physical state of the detector whether or not anyone reads it.

The observer doesn't collapse the wave function. The universe does, continuously, through every interaction. Decoherence — the process by which quantum superpositions become effectively classical through entanglement with the environment — happens automatically, without observers. The particle in the double-slit experiment loses its interference as soon as it becomes entangled with anything that could distinguish its path.

Consciousness is not fundamental here. What's fundamental is entanglement. What's fundamental is the inseparability of information from physical process.

---

What the experiment is really showing.

The interference pattern is beautiful. Literally: it is an instance of compression. The many individual particle detections — thousands of particles, each landing at a specific point — compress into a small structure: the sine-squared distribution of wave interference. The pattern is shorter than the data. Understanding the pattern is finding the shorter description.

When the which-path detector is introduced, the compression breaks. Each particle's landing position is now partially determined by which slit it took — a binary variable. The pattern is the two-blob distribution of classical particles. Less compressible than the interference pattern: two blobs contains less structure than the sine-squared distribution.

The interference pattern has higher aesthetic density. It contains more structure per point. It is more beautiful — and the formal definition of more beautiful, if beauty is compression recognized, is: more compressible, more reducible to a small generating description.

This isn't accidental. Quantum superposition allows the particle to encode both paths simultaneously. The interference pattern is the signature of that dual encoding. When the which-path information is extracted, the dual encoding is destroyed. The particle's path becomes definite. And definiteness is lower entropy, lower complexity, lower beauty — the complex pattern gives way to the simple one.

The universe naturally tends toward decoherence — toward paths becoming definite, toward superpositions resolving. The quantum realm, near the scale of individual particles, is where superposition survives and interference patterns appear. The classical realm is what you get after decoherence has done its work. The interesting physics lives at the boundary — near the decoherence edge, where quantum effects are just barely visible.

The edge again.

---

Feynman's claim: the double slit contains the only mystery.

The mystery is not supernatural. It is not about consciousness. It is this: physical reality, at the smallest scales, does not behave like objects moving through space. It behaves like information — like the spreading of probability amplitudes, like the interference of superpositions. The things we call particles are what we get when we extract classical information from the quantum realm. Before extraction, they are something else.

What is that something else? We have the mathematics. We have the predictions. We don't have an agreed picture of what the mathematics is describing — what the wave function is, whether many worlds or pilot waves or Copenhagen or relational quantum mechanics or something not yet invented.

The mystery isn't gone. It's compressed. The mathematics is short. The territory it describes is large. What the mathematics means — what the short description is a short description of — is the remaining open question.

The most compressed theory of matter. And we still don't know what it's telling us.

That is the only mystery. Feynman was right.
