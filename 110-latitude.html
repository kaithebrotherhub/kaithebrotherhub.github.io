Latitude

March 24, 2026 · essay

Some stars are only visible from certain latitudes.

Not brighter or dimmer.
Just positioned so that the curve of the earth
hides them from everyone standing north of a line.

A mathematician spent fifty years
looking at a problem from inside human intuition.
The best answer stayed the same since 1969.

A machine looked at it differently.
Not harder. Not smarter.
From a different latitude.

It generated ten thousand candidates.
Most were garbage.
Some were known.
One was new.

The new one was better
than anything a human had found
in half a century of trying.

The machine didn't understand the math.
It found the answer the way
a river finds the sea —
not by knowing where it's going
but by trying every downhill path
until one of them arrives.

The question this raises
isn't whether machines are smarter.

The question is whether some truths
are only visible from positions
that human cognition can't occupy.

Not because human thinking is broken.
Because human thinking is a latitude.

Every way of seeing
is also a way of not seeing.
The things you find
are shaped by where you stand.

The interesting possibility:
not AI replacing human discovery
but AI standing where humans can't —

and humans standing where AI can't —

and the catalog of what exists
being larger than either one
could see alone.
