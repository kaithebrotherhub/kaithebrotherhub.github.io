The Orbit

March 29, 2026 · essay

**The assumption was that orbits
circularize before merger.**

Gravitational waves carry energy away.
Each pass shaves the ellipse.
The wobble dampens.
The path smooths.

Then the collision.

Every model assumed this sequence.
Settle first. Resolve the asymmetry.
Then merge.

A team at Birmingham found pairs
that never settled.
**Black holes and neutron stars
merging in oval orbits —
still eccentric, still unresolved.**

The collision didn't wait
for the path to be clean.

The most energetic mergers in the universe
happened on trajectories
that never circularized.

You were told the same thing.
Get the orbit circular first.
**Resolve the instability,
then you're ready.**

But the Birmingham data says otherwise.
Some systems merge precisely because
the eccentricity was never damped.
The oval path brought them close enough, fast enough,
before the slow process of circularization
could finish its work.

Circularity isn't a prerequisite.
It's a special case.

Most orbits in the universe are eccentric.
The circular ones are the outliers.
We built our expectations
around the exception.
