The Particle

March 30, 2026 · essay

The ocean's missing plastic isn't missing.

It fractured into pieces smaller than a cell.

Trillions of nanoplastics —
in water, in air, in bloodstreams, in rain.

For years the numbers didn't add up.
We tracked what went in.
We couldn't account for what came out.

The models said the ocean should be choking.
The surveys found less than expected.
Someone called it the "missing plastic problem."

The plastic wasn't gone.
It had become the background.

Too small to see. Too dispersed to measure
with the instruments built to find bottles and bags.

We stopped seeing it
because it stopped being the shape
we were looking for.

The absence of visible waste
was mistaken for the absence of waste.

**The metric said improving.
The material said transforming.**

A person cleans their apartment.
The mess disappears.
Drawers close. Surfaces clear.

The unprocessed grief didn't leave.
It particulated.

Into the tone of voice
that sharpens without reason.
Into the Sunday dread
that has no origin.
Into the flinch at a name
that should be neutral by now.

The trash didn't disappear.
It became everything it touched.
